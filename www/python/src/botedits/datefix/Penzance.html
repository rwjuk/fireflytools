t38About 400 prehistoric stone axes, known as Group 1 axes and made from [[Elvan|greenstone]], have been found all over Britain, which from [[petrological]] analysis appear to come from west Cornwall.<ref>Markham, M. & Floyd, P. A. (1998) "Geochemical Fingerprinting of West Cornish Greenstones as an aid to Provenancing Neolithic Axes". Read at the Annual Conference of the Ussher Society, January 1998</ref> Although the quarry has not been identified, it has been suggested that the ''Gear'', a rock now submerged half a mile from the shore at Penzance, may be the site. A significant amount of trade is indicated as many have been found elsewhere in Britain.<ref name="Pool">Pool, P. A. S. (1974) ''The History of the Town and Borough of Penzance.'' Penzance: The Corporation of Penzance.</ref> The earliest evidence of settlement in Penzance is from the [[Bronze Age]]. A number of bronze implements such as a [[palstave]], a spear-head, a [[knife]], and [[Pin|pins]], along with much pottery and large quantities of charcoal were discovered when building a new housing estate, at Tredarvah, to the west of Alverton.<ref>"Excavation News, 1963–1964: Tredarvah, Penzance". ''Cornish Archaeology.'' {{date}} p. 85</ref> The defensive earthwork known as [[Lescudjack Hill Fort|Lescudjack Castle]] is not excavated, but almost certainly belongs to the [[Iron Age]].<ref>[[Craig Weatherhill]] ''Cornovia: Ancient Sites of Cornwall & Scilly'' (Alison Hodge 1985; Halsgrove 1997, 2000)</ref> A single rampart encloses three acres of hilltop, and would have dominated the approach to the area from the east. There are no signs of the additional ramparts reported by [[William Hals]] in about 1730,<ref name="Pool" /> and the site is now surrounded by housing with allotments. Excavations in 2008, {{convert|1|km}} to the west at [[Penwith College]] found an enclosure ditch and pottery indicating a settlement, and an evolving field system with ditches and interconnecting pits suggesting water management.<ref>{{Cite book |last=Gossip |first=James |title=Penwith College, Penzance Cornwall. Archaeological recording in advance of redevelopment: Archive report |last2=Thorpe, Carl |publisher=Cornwall Council |year=2010 |location=Truro}}</ref> There are traces of a rampart and ditch to the west of Penzance at Mount Misery, and an [[Lesingey Round|oval rampart and ditch at Lesingey]] above the [[St Just in Penwith|St Just]] road, which together with Lescudjack, overlook the coast of Penzance and Newlyn.<ref>Russell, V. 40Until recently, there was little evidence for anything but an early and short Roman occupation of Cornwall, and there have so far been only three finds in Penzance. In August 1899 two coins of [[Vespasian]] (69–79 AD) were found in an ancient trench in Penzance Cemetery.<ref>{{Cite web |title=Coin Roman Reign of Vespasian |url=https://www.penleehouse.org.uk/collections/item/PEZPH:1975.653 |access-date=14 October 2018 |website=Penlee House: Gallery and Museum, Penzanace}}</ref><ref>{{Cite web |title=Coin of The Reign of Vespasian |url=https://www.penleehouse.org.uk/collections/item/PEZPH:1975.652 |access-date=14 October 2018 |website=Penlee House: Gallery and Museum, Penzance}}</ref> The coins were eight feet below ground together with some cow bones, and are now in the [[Penlee House|Penlee House Museum]]. Another coin, found in 1934 in the Alverton area, depicts [[Sol (Roman mythology)|Sol]], the Roman sun god.<ref name="PenleeH">{{Cite web |title=Roman Coin |url=https://www.penleehouse.org.uk/collections/item/PEZPH:1975.651 |access-date=14 October 2018 |website=Penlee House: Gallery and Museum, Penzance}}</ref> It is described as a ″''coin of the reign of Constantine the Great''″, and was also donated to the museum.<ref name="PenleeH" /> A 30&nbsp;mm (1 3/16&nbsp;in)'' [[sestertius]]'' was found on a building site in or around Penzance about ten years previously, and was presented to the [[Royal Institution of Cornwall]].<ref>Penhallurick, R. D. (2009) ''Ancient an Early Medieval Coins from Cornwall and Scilly.'' London: Royal Numismatic Society.</ref> Larger quantities of Roman coins have been found nearby, at [[Marazion Marsh]] and [[Kerris]] in Paul parish, but there is no evidence of any Roman settlement in the area, although nearby villages such as [[Chysauster]] were occupied at this time.<ref>{{Cite web |title=Roman coin |url=https://www.penleehouse.org.uk/collections/item/PEZPH:1975.650 |access-date=14 October 2018 |website=Penlee House: Gallery and Museum, Penzance}}</ref><ref>Haverfield, F. & Taylor, M.
